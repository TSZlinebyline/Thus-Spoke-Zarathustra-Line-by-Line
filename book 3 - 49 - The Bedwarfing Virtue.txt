[3_49:1] When Zarathustra was again on the continent, he did not go straightway to his mountains and his cave, but made many wanderings and questionings, and ascertained this and that; so that he said of himself jestingly: ōLo, a river that floweth back unto its source in many windings!ö 1
	He came back to dry land, wandered around, and questioned people. He is trying to understand this and that. Then joked to himself that he is like a river that flowed back to his source. That is cute symbolism because rivers pour out into the ocean. He is working backwards. Except that there is no true source, only the closed circuit of an impossible river. 
	SPEC: Possible connection to the Greek legend of Oceanus. The Greek God who controlled a world-spanning river, which flowed back on itself. Beyond the river lay the entrance to the underworld and the Blessed Isles. Zarathustra has just come back from the Isles, where some of his followers were. Additionally, Odysseus in the Odyssey Book 11 had to cross the world-spanning river to enter the underworld. Next line,

	There is a timeline question here. The reader might be wondering why Zarathustra thought much could have changed in a small amount of time that had passed. [3_49:11] Here am I still like a cock in a strange farm-yard, at which even the hens peck: but on that account I am not unfriendly to the hens. 
	Earlier in the book he was clear that he does not believe in turning the other cheek. See section 19 The Bite of the Adder. He lists two relevant to this passage points. The first that failure to acknowledge harm from an enemy leads to your enemy and yourself being humiliated. It is better for both parties for a small revenge in his mind. The second when you deal with a person who is often alone with deep thoughts and hurt them in any way. They are not ever capable of forgiveness because they are not capable of forgetting. With that summary there are a few possibilities here:
	1. He isnÆt following his own advice and he pays for it with his bitterness at the end of this section. He didnÆt return blow for blow so it turned inward and as the section indicates (combined with the next one) he was an anchorite and the stone went to the bottom. 	2. He considers the people there to be below a normal enemy, not even worth striking back. His argument for blow for blow is that it is humiliating to know you didnÆt even hurt the person you intended to hurt, so give them some dignity. These people are so small in his view they deserve nothing.
	3. He is rationalizing his position, or maybe moralizing the situation. Zarathustra just came off a boat friendless and is following a river. He isnÆt even alone in his cave, he is in a strange farmyard where everyone is his boss.
